Introduction

This language, natively known as фə Kəláw yóbəl, is a conlang made for the Conlang Toiletia Discord server reconstruction challenge, between June 22 and 25. It is the proto-language for Team Blue's languages, and the target for reconstruction for Team Red.

This document describes the basics of the language, from its phonology and phonetics to morphophonology, morphology and syntax, and finishes with a short word-list. Since the conlang was made on such short notice, this grammar is obviously an incomplete description of the language. Expect some things to not be fully explained and some gaps left unfilled.

This is also where you come in: there is room for you to innovate your own words, grammar etc. as you derive your languages and make life difficult for the opposing team ;)

The language has a fairly poor consonant inventory, with 11 phonemic consonants /m n t' t k ʔ ф ts' s r j/ and three allophonic consonant segments [v w z]. The obstruents, except for /ф/, tend to form pairs of glottalisation. The following consonants pattern together: /t' t/, /ʔ k/ and /ts' s/. For the purposes of pho­no­tactics, the phoneme /ts'/ is treated as two consonants or a cluster, but the glottalised /s/ does not appear without the accompanying prestopping.

Consonants can cluster rather freely, and the maximum cluster is two consonants. Clusters are allowed initially, medially and finally. The primary restrictions are either that the cluster must form a geminate sequence, or that the two consonants are as distinct from eachother as they can be: they must not share either place or manner of articulation. Other than this, there are no positional restrictions: practically any cluster can form anywhere, including initial geminates.

The consonant /n/ behaves as though it is both a dental and dorsal for the purpose of morphophonological processes. Phonetically, it is always a dental nasal [n].

There are four phonemic vowels, listed here as /a i ə o/. They come in pairs of high /i o/ and low /a ə/; this determines their behaviour in morpho­pho­nolo­gical processes. There is no phonemic vowel [u] anywhere in the language, and the higher back vowel always appears as [o], thus leaving it to be represented with /o/.

Vowels can be either modally or markedly phonated. The marked voicing was probably creaky, though realisations could range from creaky, breathy, nasalised slack voice etc. Its distinctive feature is that it is somehow different from modal or tenuis voicing. Phonemically, it is represented with a noncommittant underdot diacritic: /ạ/.

Phonemically, stress or pitch were not distinctive in the underlying form of the language. The second-to-last phonemic vowel in a word is always the accented one. This is complicated by morpho­phono­logical vowel processes, which can obscure accent placement rules and cause the accent to occur "out of place" or even appear as if it were actually distinctive.

Morphophonology

There are two important morphophonological processes in the language: consonant dissimilation and vowel sandhi.

Consonant Dissimilation

Consonant dissimilation is a pervasive process that regulates consonant clusters within a phonological word. Since a cluster must be heterorganic and heteromethodic, this means that at least one of the consonants must change its manner or place of articulation. There are no possible clusters where both consonants have the same place and manner of articulation: these are, by nature, always geminates.

Dissimilation relies on an established sonority hierarchy for manners of articulation possible for a consonant. The hierarchy, from most to least sonorous, is:

  1. Sonorant/approximant
  2. Nasal
  3. Fricative
  4. Plosive

In case of place dissimilation, the dissimilating segment is going to be the more sonorous one. In case of manner dissimilation, the dissimilating segment is in turn the one that can dissimilate into a less sonorous segment. In case that these dissimilations aren't possible (for example, no plosive can dissimilate into a less sonorous segment since plosives are the lowest on the sonority hierachy), dissimilation into a more sonorous element, or dissimilation of a less sonorous homorganic segment into a different place of articulation, is required.

Usually, consonants can dissimilate only one step away from each other; some situations necessitate a dissimilation of two or three steps so to preserve the maximal contrast requirement. Thus, we can observe the following changes:

  • |nt nk ns| > /mt mk ms/
  • |nr nj| > /mr mj/
  • |tk sф| > /sk tф/
  • |mn rj| > /фn rk/

Dissimilation does not apply across regular word borders, but it does apply to clitics, adpositions, classifiers and other tightly bound words. It is somewhat of a check of what constitutes a phonological word.

Vowel Sandhi

Vowel sandhi or perhaps assimilation is the process that resolves vowel hiatuses formed as a result of morphology. Since no vowel sequences are allowed at all in the language, sandhi processes always resolve them so that there is only one phonetic vowel remaining. Nevertheless, sandhi does not reposition the accent from where it was assigned, which indicates that the underlying phonological structure of a word is not altered.
